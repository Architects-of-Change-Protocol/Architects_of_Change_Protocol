# Reputation Constitution

**Constitution Version:** v10.0

## Constitutional purpose

**Reputation** is a governed historical reliability signal derived from constitutional records, verified behavior, claim outcomes, trust history, decision history, standing history, and dispute history. Reputation answers **what the accumulated constitutional record indicates**. Reputation is not authority, capability, policy, standing, claim, trust, verification, or decision.

## Sovereign reputation rule

A participant may have standing, claims, trust, and verification, but only a reputation type cataloged in `REPUTATION-AUTHORITIES.md`, derived from governed sources under its sources policy using an authorized calculation method, maintained through the constitutional lifecycle, and decaying per its decay policy may constitute a governed reputation signal.

The constitutional chain is:

`Authority → Capability → Policy → Standing → Claim → Trust → Verification → Reputation → Decision → Action`

Reputation may influence a decision only as a traceable historical reliability input. It never replaces required standing, evidence, policy coverage, trust evaluation, verification requirements, decision authority, or explainability, and it never directly authorizes action.

## Reputation legitimacy

A reputation signal is legitimate only when all of the following are traceable:

1. a canonical reputation authority and owner;
2. governed sources satisfying the source policy for that reputation type;
3. a valid calculation method with documented weighting and aggregation;
4. a valid lifecycle path through `Pending Source Evaluation` before `Active`;
5. every decay event, suspension, dispute, correction, revocation, and retirement; and
6. the current reputation score separately from preserved historical records.

No reputation may become `Active` without source evaluation and calculation. No reputation may override standing, capability, policy, claim evidence, or verification requirements.

## Constitutional properties

Reputation is source-backed, calculation-governed, purpose-bound, non-transferable, time-aware, decaying where cataloged, disputable where cataloged, correctable where cataloged, revocable where cataloged, historically preserved, and constitutionally evolvable. A reputation signal never copies status from one subject, context, claim, or authority to another.

## Trust ≠ Reputation and Verification ≠ Reputation

Trust, verification, and reputation are constitutionally independent:

| Trust | Verification | Reputation | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| Low | No | Low | Weak participant signal |
| High | No | Low | Trusted but unreliable history |
| Low | Yes | Low | Verified but poor history |
| High | Yes | Low | Strong current signal; poor history |
| Low | No | High | Poor current signal; strong history |
| High | No | High | Trusted; strong history; unverified |
| Low | Yes | High | Verified; strong history; low current trust |
| High | Yes | High | Fully consistent participant signal |

All eight states are constitutionally valid. Decision authorities may consume trust, verification, and reputation as independent inputs.

## Reputation is an input, not a bypass

Reputation may influence decisions. Reputation may not override:

- Authority
- Capability
- Policy
- Standing
- Claim Evidence
- Verification Requirements

Reputation is a governed input to decision-making. It is never a constitutional bypass.

## Scope boundary

This framework governs reputation legitimacy and constitutional records only. It does not implement a reputation, scoring, trust, verification, voting, federation, governance, policy, decision, claims, authentication, or authorization runtime.
